China → Southeast Asia

What I know about this corridor, written from where I actually stand — and honest about where my hands-on experience ends.

Where I stand

Vietnam is the lane I run myself. The rest of the region I know through how cargo moves.

My hands-on Southeast Asia lane is China → Vietnam — Hai Phong and Ho Chi Minh (Cat Lai), usually arriving in under a week. Most weeks that is small appliances and project cargo. The wider region — Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia and the Philippines — I know mainly through two things: how cargo routes through it, since so much of it transships at Singapore or Port Klang, and general practitioner knowledge of the main gateways and their compliance quirks. On this page I keep the line clear between the lane I work directly and the region I know from routing and experience, so you are never guessing about what is behind a claim.

The routing reality

One of the shortest deep-sea corridors — with a lot happening at the hubs.

From South China, Southeast Asia is close. Direct services to the major gateways are frequent, and intra-Asia feeder networks are dense, so transit to the main hubs is often only one to two weeks, with Vietnam usually under one. This cargo also runs east and south, nowhere near the Red Sea, so it is insulated from the disruptions affecting Europe-bound lanes. The complexity is not distance — it is the transshipment step. For smaller or secondary ports, your box is frequently consolidated onto a mainline vessel to Singapore or Port Klang, then moved onward by feeder. That extra handling is where time and risk accumulate, and where it pays to know the routing before you book.

The ports

The gateways and hubs across the corridor.

01

Singapore

Region's transshipment hub

One of the busiest transshipment ports in the world and the pivot much of the region's cargo passes through. Even when Singapore is not your final stop, your box may well move through it on the way.

02

Port Klang & Tanjung Pelepas (Malaysia)

Hub + gateway

Malaysia's main container gateways and major transshipment alternatives to Singapore. Strong onward connectivity makes them a common consolidation point for the wider region.

03

Laem Chabang (Thailand)

Gateway · general knowledge

Thailand's principal deep-sea port, serving Bangkok and the eastern industrial corridor. Certain product categories fall under Thai standards (TISI), so confirm requirements before shipping.

04

Tanjung Priok / Jakarta (Indonesia)

Gateway · general knowledge

Indonesia's largest port. Outer-island destinations usually go via transshipment, and Indonesia's SNI standards and import-licensing rules need planning ahead of the cargo, not after it lands.

05

Manila & Cebu (Philippines)

Gateways · general knowledge

The main Philippine gateways. As an archipelago, the domestic leg matters as much as the sea leg, so the final-mile plan is part of the routing decision.

06

Ho Chi Minh (Cat Lai) & Hai Phong (Vietnam)

The lane I know hands-on

My working Southeast Asia lane, covered in depth on its own page — see China → Vietnam. FCL, FOB nominee, usually under a week.

Documents & conformity

Each market has its own standards — check before you ship.

Southeast Asia is not one rulebook but several. Indonesia enforces SNI standards and import licensing for many goods; Thailand applies TISI standards to certain categories; Malaysia requires SIRIM approval for regulated products; the Philippines uses its own product-standards mark; and Singapore, though largely a free port, still controls strategic and restricted goods. None of this is difficult on its own — the failures come from assuming a product is unregulated when it isn't, and discovering it only at the destination. The practical rule is the same one that works everywhere: confirm the product-specific certification for your specific market, and arrange it at origin before the vessel sails.

Common questions

About this corridor.

Which Southeast Asia lane do you actually run yourself?

China → Vietnam, to Hai Phong and Ho Chi Minh (Cat Lai). The wider region I know through routing and general practitioner knowledge, and I'll be clear about where my hands-on experience ends.

Why does so much Southeast Asia cargo route through Singapore or Port Klang?

They are the region's transshipment backbones. For smaller or secondary ports, cargo is often consolidated onto a mainline vessel to one of these hubs and then moved onward by feeder — an extra handling step worth planning around.

How long does ocean freight from China to Southeast Asia take?

It is a short corridor — often around one to two weeks to the main hubs, with Vietnam usually under a week. Secondary ports take longer when transshipment is involved.
